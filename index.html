Behavioral & Experimental Economics

Charlotte Saucet

Associate Professor (Maître de conférences) at the Centre d'Économie de la Sorbonne, Université Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne.

I am an economist working at the intersection of behavioral and experimental economics. My research focuses on how individuals form and update beliefs — with a particular interest in motivated reasoning, memory biases, and strategic communication.

Published & Forthcoming

Is motivated memory (just) a matter of mood?
with A. Prati · Experimental Economics · 2025

Tests whether overconfidence driven by biased memory is explained by self-enhancement or mood-congruency effects. Results support the self-enhancement hypothesis: individuals better recall positive than negative feedback even when in a negative mood.

PDF →
Motivated Skepticism
with J. Hagenbach · Review of Economic Studies · 2025

Experimental study of how individuals read strategically-transmitted information when they have preferences over what they will learn. Finds that skepticism is lower when it is self-threatening — a new channel for belief protection in communication.

PDF → Appendix →
The causal effect of a health treatment on beliefs, stated preferences and memories
with A. Prati · Journal of Health Economics · 2024

Exploits a natural experiment from the UK COVID-19 vaccination campaign. After vaccination, individuals update beliefs overly optimistically about their vaccine's safety and effectiveness, and show distorted memories of prior beliefs.

Appendix →
Unethical Amnesia Responds more to Instrumental than to Hedonic Motives
with F. Galeotti and M.C. Villeval · PNAS · 2020

Large-scale incentivized online experiment (n=1,322). Hedonic considerations alone do not motivate forgetting of past unethical behavior, but when forgetting can justify a future action, motivated forgetting becomes more likely.

PDF → Appendix →
Motivated Memory in Dictator Games
with M.C. Villeval · Games and Economic Behavior · 2019

Experiment showing dictators remember their altruistic choices more than selfish ones. The recall asymmetry disappears when options are selected randomly by computer, suggesting a self-serving memory bias.
